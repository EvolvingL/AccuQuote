How to Win More Quotes: 7 Things That Make a Quote Win — and 3 That Make You Lose

AccuQuote Team • 2 April 2025 • 5 min read

Most trades businesses convert somewhere between 30% and 50% of their quotes. The ones at the top end — the tradespeople who are genuinely busy, who have work lined up three months out, who turn down jobs they don't want — aren't necessarily cheaper. They're not doing better work, in most cases. They're just doing the process around the quote better than everyone else.

After working with hundreds of UK tradespeople, we've identified the factors that consistently separate quotes that win from quotes that don't. Most of them aren't about price.

The 7 Things That Make a Quote Win

1

Speed — the quote arrives on the same day

The single biggest factor in winning quotes is how quickly the customer receives it. A customer who has had three tradespeople walk round their house is making a comparison — not just on price, but on who they trust to actually show up and deliver. If you send a professional, detailed quote within two hours of leaving the site visit, you signal that you're organised, that you care about the job, and that you'll probably manage the project the same way. The tradesperson who sends it three days later — even if the quote is marginally cheaper — loses before the comparison starts. The best tradespeople in this regard use apps like AccuQuote to send the quote from the van before they've even driven away.

2

An itemised breakdown rather than a single number

A single number invites negotiation. An itemised breakdown invites understanding. When a customer can see that £1,400 is 5 days of your labour, £800 is materials (with specific products listed), and £65 is the certification fee, they're not thinking about which number to haggle — they're thinking about which quote is most trustworthy. Itemisation also protects you: if the customer later claims the quote was "just for labour" or "didn't include materials", you have a document that says exactly the opposite.

3

A professional PDF, not a text message or handwritten note

How you present your quote is a proxy for how you'll present yourself on the job. A clean, well-formatted PDF with your business name, your contact details, a clear breakdown and a validity date tells the customer you take your business seriously. A WhatsApp message with a figure sends the opposite signal — regardless of your actual quality of work.

4

Clarity on what's included — and what isn't

Customers worry about surprises. A quote that clearly states what is and isn't included is a quote that's easy to say yes to. "Price includes all materials, labour, waste removal and certification. Does not include making good of decorating after works." This one paragraph prevents the most common post-job dispute in the trades and it makes the customer feel safe in accepting your quote.

5

A specific start date

"I can start Monday the 14th" wins jobs that "I'll be in touch about availability" loses. Customers doing home improvements have usually been thinking about the project for months. They want it done. A tradesperson who can give them a specific date — even if it's four weeks away — is easier to say yes to than a vague promise of availability.

6

Evidence of previous work

A quote that links to a Checkatrade or Google profile with genuine reviews from real customers is worth more than any discount you can offer. A customer who can read five reviews saying "turned up on time, left the site clean, did exactly what he said" feels safe. If you're not actively collecting reviews after every job, you're making every future quote harder to win.

7

A follow-up call or message after three days

Most tradespeople send a quote and wait. The ones who win more follow up. Not aggressively — a simple "Hi, just checking you received my quote and happy to answer any questions" text or call three days after sending converts a meaningful percentage of quotes that would otherwise go cold. Customers get busy, forget, or are waiting for another quote that hasn't arrived. A follow-up often tips the balance.

The 3 Things That Make You Lose

Sending the quote too late

A quote that arrives four or five days after the site visit has usually already lost. The customer has mentally moved on to the tradesperson who responded faster. Even if your price is better, timing your quote badly signals poor responsiveness — and responsiveness is the single thing customers worry about most when hiring a tradesperson.

A vague or poorly formatted quote

"Work as discussed — £1,850" is not a quote. It's a number with no context, no protection for you or the customer, and no reason to choose you over someone else. Vague quotes get rejected or endlessly negotiated. Detailed quotes get accepted because they demonstrate competence and protect both parties.

Pitching below your worth to "win" the job

The customers who push hardest on price are often the most difficult to work for. Cutting your rate to win a price-sensitive customer usually means absorbing extra requests and scope creep that weren't in the original quote. The tradespeople with the healthiest businesses are often not the cheapest — they're the ones who quote accurately and confidently and let some jobs go. Winning the wrong jobs at the wrong price is worse than not winning them at all.

The simple truth about quote conversion: You probably can't control whether you're the cheapest. You can absolutely control whether you're the fastest, the most professional, and the most thorough. Those factors win jobs at full price. Being cheap wins jobs at reduced margins.

Putting It Into Practice

The tradespeople who implement even half of the above will see their conversion rate improve significantly. The common thread through all of it is professionalism — not the slick marketing kind, but the genuine operational kind. Turn up when you say you will. Send the quote the same day. Make it clear what's included. Follow up once.
