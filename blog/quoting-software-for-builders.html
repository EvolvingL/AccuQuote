Quoting Software for Builders: The Complete Guide to Pricing Building Work in the UK

AccuQuote Team • 22 January 2025 • 8 min read
Timber is up 41%. Plasterboard is up 28%. Cement is up 19%. If your quote template is from 2022, you're losing money on every job — not because you're bad at your trade, but because your numbers are out of date.

Of all the trades, builders produce the most complex quotes. An electrician quotes circuits and accessories. A plasterer quotes rooms and bags of plaster. A builder quotes a project that might span six or eight weeks, involve three or four different trades, require materials from half a dozen suppliers, need plant hire for a skip and a mixer, include groundworks that can't be fully scoped until you've dug, and carry a genuine risk of hitting something unexpected in a Victorian terraced house that was last touched in 1987.

It's not surprising that building quotes go wrong more often than quotes in any other trade. What is surprising is how little good quoting software exists that's actually designed for how builders work — particularly smaller builders and sole traders who are doing their own quoting without a QS or estimator behind them.

AccuQuote was designed with over 200 UK tradesmen — builders, electricians, plasterers, plumbers — who were all losing money the same way: quoting from memory while material prices kept moving. This guide covers why building quotes go wrong, what good quoting software needs to handle, and how to quote faster and more accurately in 2025.

Why Building Quotes Are Hard to Get Right

There are a few structural reasons why builders consistently struggle with quoting, beyond just "it's complicated":

Multi-trade coordination

Most building projects touch multiple trades — foundations and structure, first and second fix carpentry, plastering, electrics, plumbing, decorating. If you're managing subbies, you need their rates in the quote. If you're doing it all yourself across multiple disciplines, you need to price each phase correctly. Generic quoting software doesn't have a concept of "electrics phase" or "plastering phase" — it's just line items.

Unknowns and contingencies

Every experienced builder knows that the first thing you discover when you open a wall is something that changes the project. Buried pipework running through a structural wall. Damp behind the render. A joist that's half rotten. The question isn't whether there will be surprises — it's whether your quote accounts for them. Most don't. Most builders absorb these costs or have difficult conversations with customers at the worst possible moment.

Materials price volatility

Timber, plasterboard, insulation, cement, steel — all moved substantially in price between 2021 and 2024 and continue to fluctuate. A quote produced with prices from three months ago may already be wrong. If you're buying materials from Travis Perkins, Jewson, or BuildBase, you need current prices in your quote, not last year's.

VAT complexity

New builds attract zero-rated VAT on labour. Conversions and renovations are complex — sometimes reduced rate applies, sometimes not. If you're not VAT-registered and quoting a job where the customer is expecting to claim VAT back on a renovation, it's a conversation you want to have upfront, not at invoice stage.

The margin killer: In a BuildBase survey of small builders, the average project overrun on labour was 18% and on materials was 12%. That's not waste — it's unquoted scope. The fix isn't to work faster. It's to quote more accurately from the start.

What Builders Need from Quoting Software

A quoting tool that actually works for building projects needs to handle:

  • Phased quoting. Break the project into phases — demolition and strip-out, groundworks, structure, first fix, second fix, finish — and price each phase separately. This gives the customer a clear picture of the project and gives you a framework for progress payments.
  • Multi-trade line items. Labour should be priceable by trade: builder rate (£250–350/day), electrician subbie (£280–320/day), plumber subbie (£250–300/day), plasterer subbie (£200–260/day). These are different costs and should be shown separately.
  • Plant hire and skip costs. A skip costs £250–400 in most of England depending on size and location. A mixer hire is £80–120/week from Brandon Hire or HSS. These always get forgotten.
  • Contingency line. A 10–15% contingency on a building project is standard practice, not padding. Present it explicitly in the quote as a contingency line rather than burying it in labour margins. Customers who understand what contingency means don't argue about it — it's the ones who feel surprised by costs who cause problems.
  • VAT treatment. Whether you're VAT registered or not, the quote needs to handle this correctly.
  • Real materials pricing. From Travis Perkins, Jewson, Wickes, or Toolstation. Not made-up ballpark figures.

Pricing Different Types of Building Work

Extensions (single and double storey)

Single-storey rear extensions in the UK typically run from £1,500–2,500/m² depending on spec, location and roof type. A flat-roof extension is cheaper than pitched. Add 20–30% for a full kitchen fit-out within the extension. Always price groundworks separately — they're the biggest variable.

Full house refurbishments

A typical full refurb (replaster, rewire, replumb, new kitchen and bathroom) on a 3-bed Victorian terrace runs £60,000–120,000 depending on spec and location. The key is phasing: structure and services first, then plastering, then second fix, then decoration. Trying to start decoration while services work is still live creates rework and bad feeling.

Garage and loft conversions

Loft conversions range from £35,000 (basic dormer with Velux rooflights) to £80,000+ (full hip-to-gable with full height headroom and en-suite). Garage conversions are simpler — typically £15,000–25,000 for a full habitable conversion with insulation, new floor, plastering, glazing and electrics. Always confirm permitted development vs. planning permission status before quoting.
