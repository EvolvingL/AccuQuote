Quoting Software for Electricians: How to Quote Any Job in 20 Minutes

AccuQuote Team • 12 March 2025 • 7 min read
Copper is up 35% since 2020. Twin and earth, containment, accessories — all moved. If you're still quoting from memory or last year's spreadsheet, you're not just slow — you're losing margin on every job.

Ask any electrician what they hate most about running their business and the answer is almost always the same: the quoting. Not the work itself — the endless time spent after a site visit, back at the kitchen table, trying to remember what you said you'd include, looking up cable prices, calculating how many back boxes you need for a 14-socket first fix, and then wondering if you've priced the labour right.

For most sole traders and small electrical firms, quoting takes between two and four hours per job. A busy sparks doing four or five surveys a week can easily spend a full working day every week just on quotes — time that isn't billable, isn't paid, and often doesn't even convert to a job.

AccuQuote was built with over 200 UK tradesmen — electricians included — who were all dealing with the same problem: materials moving while their quote templates stood still. This guide covers what makes electrical quoting genuinely hard, what good software needs to do, and how to quote faster without losing accuracy.

Why Electricians Waste So Much Time Quoting

The core problem with electrical quoting is the number of variables. A kitchen rewire isn't just "X days labour plus materials" — it's consumer unit type, ways in the board, circuit runs, cable type and length for each circuit, back boxes, faceplates, containment, accessories, test certificate, EIC, Part P notification and so on. Miss any one of them and you're either eating the cost or having an awkward conversation with the customer.

Most electricians handle this one of three ways, and none of them are great:

  • Spreadsheet templates — good if you built them yourself and remember to update them, but they're rigid and don't travel well to different job types.
  • Rough mental arithmetic on-site, written up later — fast, but prone to forgetting things. How many metres of 2.5mm T&E did you actually walk? What was the run to the outdoor socket?
  • Quoting software designed for builders — clunky for electrical work, doesn't know the difference between a radial and a ring final, and almost certainly doesn't have CEF or Screwfix SKUs built in.

The result is quotes that take too long and still aren't quite right. Either you've underpriced to be competitive and you're working for less than your day rate, or you've padded to be safe and you're losing jobs to someone who quoted more accurately.

What Good Quoting Software for Electricians Actually Does

The right quoting tool for an electrician needs to understand electrical work specifically. That means it needs to:

  • Know the difference between consumer unit upgrades, rewires, partial rewires, EV charger installs, solar and battery installs, and smart home work — and build different bill of materials templates for each
  • Calculate cable runs from room dimensions, not just from you typing "some cable"
  • Include testing and certification costs as standard — EICR, EIC, Part P notification fees — because these are always forgotten and always come out of your margin
  • Pull real materials costs from Screwfix, CEF (City Electrical Factors), or Toolstation, not generic placeholder prices that don't reflect what you'll actually pay
  • Let you specify your day rate and automatically calculate labour by job type and scope
  • Output a clean, professional PDF that looks like it came from a proper business, not a Word document from 2008

The certification trap: A domestic EIC currently costs around £35–50 to register with your certification body. A Part P notification is £50–75 depending on your local authority. On a small job, that's a meaningful chunk of margin — and it's almost always forgotten when quoting quickly. Good software includes it automatically.

Beyond materials, the tool needs to handle the awkward in-betweens: what to charge when you find the existing installation is in worse shape than quoted, how to present options (standard vs. upgrade), and how to structure a quote so the customer can understand what they're paying for without it reading like a parts list from a technical manual.

What to Look for in Quoting Software for Electricians

There's no shortage of quoting tools on the market, but most are built for general tradespeople or construction — not specifically for electrical work. Here's what separates a good tool from a generic one:

  • Trade-specific templates. Does it understand the difference between a EICR and a full rewire? Does it know to include testing fees?
  • Live materials pricing. Generic quoting tools use placeholder prices. You need current prices from the suppliers you actually use — Screwfix, CEF, Toolstation, Rexel.
  • Labour calculation by scope. Not just "enter your day rate" — but a sensible estimate of days based on job type and scope, which you can adjust.
  • Speed on site. If you can't complete the quote before you leave the customer's house, it's not going to happen until 10pm. The tool needs to work on a phone, quickly, in real-world conditions.
  • Professional output. The PDF the customer receives is a reflection of your business. It needs to look professional — branded, structured, clear.
  • VAT handling. If you're VAT registered, this needs to be automatic. If you're not, it shouldn't appear.
